My personal design style has gradually become more clean, structured, and user-focused. At the beginning of learning web design, I tended to focus more on visual effects and making pages look “impressive,” sometimes at the expense of clarity. Over time, I’ve shifted toward simplicity and usability. Now, I prefer minimal layouts with strong visual hierarchy, consistent spacing, and clear navigation. I also pay more attention to readability and accessibility rather than decorative elements. My current style could be described as modern, minimal, and purpose-driven, where every design choice has a functional reason behind it.

This evolution happened as I learned more about user experience and accessibility. Earlier projects often felt more experimental and inconsistent, but feedback and practice helped me realize that good design is not about adding more, but about removing unnecessary complexity. I’ve become more confident in using whitespace, consistent typography, and grid-based layouts to create a more professional and polished look.

One of the highlights of learning web design for me was seeing a project come together from concept to a fully working website. There’s something very satisfying about turning an idea into something interactive that other people can actually use. I especially enjoyed the problem-solving side of design—figuring out why something wasn’t aligning properly, or how to make a layout responsive across different devices. That moment when everything finally works as intended felt really rewarding because it showed clear progress and skill development.

If I had more time, one thing I would improve is adding more advanced interactivity and backend functionality to my projects. Most of my work focused on front-end design, but I would like to include features like user accounts, dynamic content, or databases. This would make my websites feel more like real-world applications rather than static pages, and it would also help me develop a more complete understanding of full-stack development.

In terms of skills application, I plan to use what I’ve learned in both academic and personal projects. For example, I can apply web design principles when creating portfolios, presentations, or even small freelance-style projects. These skills are also useful in other areas like digital communication and marketing, where clear layout and visual communication are important. Long term, I could see myself using these skills in more professional contexts such as building websites for organizations or personal clients.

Looking ahead, I’m particularly interested in learning more about JavaScript frameworks, interactive UI design, and backend integration. I also want to explore performance optimization and modern web technologies that improve user experience. Additionally, I’m curious about design systems and how larger companies maintain consistency across complex websites and applications.

For someone just starting out in web design, my advice would be to focus on the basics first—structure, layout, and usability—before worrying too much about advanced effects. It’s easy to get distracted by making things look visually impressive, but a good website is first and foremost easy to use. I would also say don’t be afraid of making mistakes, because debugging and fixing issues is one of the fastest ways to learn. Finally, getting feedback early and often is incredibly valuable, because it helps you improve in ways you might not notice on your own.
